The AI-to-Spatial Logic: How the GenAI Decides What to Render

Scenario: A technician says, "Show me the coolant leak."

The system processes this request through a six-step reasoning pipeline:

STEP 1: VOICE INTENT PARSING
Input: "Show me the coolant leak"
NLP layer extracts: action = show, system = coolant, anomaly = leak
STEP 2: SENSOR CONTEXT RETRIEVAL
Live OBD-II / CAN bus query:
  Active DTCs: P0A93 (coolant temp sensor A), P1DF1 (coolant pump fault)
  Live PIDs: coolant temp = 94°C (threshold 80°C), flow rate = 1.2 L/min (nominal 3.8)
  Thermal camera: hotspot detected at rear-left quadrant, Battery Module 4
STEP 3: VIN-TO-CAD RESOLUTION
Vehicle: 2024 Toyota bZ4X, VIN JF2GTAGC5R8012345
CAD database fetch:
  Battery TMS assembly (layer: BMS_thermal_v2.3.cad)
  Coolant circuit routing (layer: coolant_loop_v1.7.cad)
  Module 4 position: X=847mm, Y=214mm, Z=320mm from front axle centerline
STEP 4: SEMANTIC LAYER SELECTION
✓ Render: coolant_loop_v1.7 (full routing, highlighted blue)
✓ Render: BMS_module_04 (pulsing red at confirmed hotspot)
✓ Render: coolant_pump_assembly (yellow — fault active)
✗ Suppress: electrical_harness, brake_lines, HVAC_cabin (not relevant)
STEP 5: GENAI NARRATIVE GENERATION
LLM output: "I am detecting low coolant flow and a thermal spike at Battery Module 4.
The most likely failure is the Module 4 inlet coupling. See the highlighted fitting
at your 2 o'clock, 18 inches in. Check for visible cracking before removing the shield."
STEP 6: SPATIAL ANCHORING
Overlay pinned to physical vehicle via SLAM and ArUco marker reference points
Depth confidence: 97.3% (LiDAR-assisted)
Overlay refresh: 60Hz, target latency under 12ms end-to-end
Key design principle: The AI does not render all data. It renders the right data. Layer suppression is as important as layer selection. Visual clutter during an active repair causes errors and slows technicians.

UI Storyboard: Coolant Leak Diagnosis Flow

Scene 1 — Vehicle Approach
Technician walks toward vehicle. HUD activates and displays: "Vehicle identified: 2024 Toyota bZ4X. 3 active DTCs detected." Fault zones appear as a soft amber glow on exterior panels.
Scene 2 — Session Start
Tech says: "Hey Aria, start diagnostic."
Aria responds: "Starting diagnostic. I see a coolant fault. Say 'show coolant' or look at the Coolant panel for 2 seconds to begin."
Scene 3 — Layer Activation
Tech looks at "Coolant System" card (eye-dwell 800ms). Card expands to full overlay. Interior of vehicle renders: blue coolant lines trace through the battery pack. Module 4 pulses red. Coolant pump glows yellow.
Scene 4 — Step-by-Step Guidance
Aria narrates the diagnosis. Step counter in corner: "Step 1 of 7." A floating 3D arrow points at the inlet coupling.
Scene 5 — Step Confirmation
Tech says: "Got it. Next step." Step advances. Voice and visual confirmation. Timer records step completion for the job card.
Scene 6 — Safety Trigger
Tech's hand moves within 200mm of high-voltage bus. All overlays dim except the HV bus, shown in pulsing red with a warning glyph. Aria: "Stop. High-voltage zone. Confirm insulated gloves before proceeding." Tech nods. System records safety acknowledgment and resumes.

Interaction guardrails:

  • No more than 3 simultaneous UI elements visible at once, preventing cognitive overload.
  • Critical safety alerts override all other interactions immediately.
  • Voice commands always take priority over eye-tracking.
  • All interactions are logged with timestamps for liability and training purposes.

Safety Logic: The HV-STOP Guardrail

Guardrail name: HV-STOP (High-Voltage Spatial Trigger and Override Protocol)

EV high-voltage systems (400V to 800V) present lethal risk. A technician distracted by the XR overlay could move their hands toward a live HV bus while visually focused on a CAD layer elsewhere in the vehicle.

LayerTechnologyFunction
Detection Depth camera (LiDAR/Time-of-Flight) and HV bus CAD position data Tracks hand and tool position in real 3D space; compares against known HV bus locations from vehicle CAD
Proximity threshold 300mm warning; 150mm hard stop 300mm triggers an amber warning ring around the HV bus. 150mm triggers a full stop gate.
Alert modality Visual (full-overlay red), audio (alarm tone), haptic (if gloves support it) Multi-modal delivery ensures the alert is not missed
Acknowledgment Verbal "HV aware" or explicit nod required Implicit acknowledgment is not accepted
Logging Every proximity event logged with technician ID, timestamp, VIN, and zone approached Creates safety audit trail required for OSHA compliance and OEM liability protection

Hard rules:

  1. HV-STOP cannot be disabled by the technician. Only a remote service manager can lift a stop gate.
  2. If HV-STOP is triggered and the technician does not acknowledge within 10 seconds, the system escalates to the service manager's dashboard.
  3. High-voltage zones are always rendered as a persistent ambient layer, even when not explicitly queried.

Managing the Gap: Latency

XR overlays must feel anchored to physical reality. When a technician moves their head, the 3D CAD layer must track the vehicle in real time. Any perceptible lag causes:

  • Nausea and simulator sickness, reported in 30 to 40% of first-time XR users at latencies above 20ms.
  • Spatial anchoring errors, where the technician reaches for the wrong component.
  • Trust collapse: if the overlay is wrong once, technicians stop trusting it entirely.

Technical reality:

  • Current generation XR headsets (HoloLens 2, Magic Leap 2): photon latency approximately 18ms in best-case local rendering.
  • With edge compute, sensor fusion, and cloud inference: end-to-end latency is realistically 25 to 40ms under load.
  • Human perceptual threshold for spatial lag: approximately 12ms (MIT Media Lab, 2020).
  • The gap: 13 to 28ms above the ideal threshold under real-world conditions.

How we manage user expectations without hiding the problem:

StrategyImplementation
Predictive rendering SLAM-based head motion prediction renders the overlay one frame ahead of actual position, reducing perceived lag to approximately 8ms in controlled tests.
Confidence indicators The overlay renders with a subtle "anchor confidence" ring: solid green above 95% SLAM lock, pulsing amber while recovering, transparent when tracking is lost.
Graceful degradation If latency exceeds 40ms, the system automatically reduces the rendered layer count and notifies the technician: "Simplified view active. Move to better lighting for full overlay."
Calibration ritual A 90-second onboarding scan when entering a bay builds a spatial map of the specific vehicle and pre-caches likely CAD layers based on open DTCs.
Honest onboarding New user orientation explicitly states: "The overlay may trail your head movement by a fraction of a second. This is normal. Trust the anchored component arrows, not the floating lines."
The goal is not to pretend latency does not exist. It is to design a UX where the latency window never lands on a safety-critical action and is clearly communicated when it occurs.

Executive Summary: Roadmap Pitch to VP of Product

To: VP of Product, Spatial Platforms Division
From: Product Management, XR Diagnostics
Subject: Why X-Ray Vision Diagnostics outcompetes a 2D mobile diagnostic app

The automotive service industry faces a structural skills crisis: 642,000 unfilled technician roles by 2027, a 23-point First-Time Fix Rate gap between ICE and EV complex repairs, and an average 23-minute wait to reach OEM expertise by phone. A 2D mobile diagnostic app solves the delivery problem: it puts a PDF in a technician's pocket. It does not solve the understanding problem.

X-Ray Vision Diagnostics solves understanding. By fusing OEM CAD data with live sensor feeds and a GenAI reasoning layer, we give technicians the spatial intelligence of a 20-year master tech without requiring one to be present. Our primary metric, First-Time Fix Rate, has a direct line to dealership profitability: every percentage point of FTFR improvement saves a 50-bay dealership approximately $180,000 annually in warranty rework, rental vehicles, and customer attrition (NADA 2024 cost modeling).

A 2D app will attract 40% of our target bays and be commoditized within 18 months as OEMs build equivalent tools themselves. A spatial platform locks in the bay, the OEM data relationship, and the technician workflow simultaneously. We have a 24-month window before the major scanner incumbents (Snap-on, Bosch) enter spatial. We should use it.
